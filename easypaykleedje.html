Role

Responsible for the design of the journey, experience and conducting user testing. Visual style of all related products (branding, app, blanket, website, manual) and implementation.

Problem to solve

While the whole country turns orange for Kingsday, kids sell old toys or their parents junk on a flea market to earn some cash. But with cash slowly disappearing, selling stuff for a couple of euros has become a bit of a hassle. How can we solve this friction and go test our solution on Kingsday in three months?

Process: Design Challenge

The creative department came together and went all-in in ideation mode. Because of the solvable frictions, PR value and connection with a physical product, Easy-pay Kleedje was picked out of 11 ideas for this year’s design challenge. From this moment we had three months to go from an abstract user journey towards testing and implementation.

Together with another designer I mapped out each step of the user journey. In Sketch, I translated parts of the journey into wireframes above. This process was quite tricky and needed close cooperation with development. Because of limited time and feasibility we had to use an external payment app Tikkie, so real payments were possible. The technical integration with Tikkie highly influenced the user experience and needed testing.

Validation of our app with kids

Once the whole flow was determined, taking technical limitations into account, it was time for the visual style. To get started I asked the help of an UI and motion designer, which guided me along the way with feedback and inspiration. After this I peer reviewed the designs with multiple colleagues for improvements. Which left me with one last challenge: the Tikkie integration.

Together with the developers we created a simple demo which I could use for testing. Via our colleagues I invited multiple kids towards our office. Interviewing kids is pretty hard, finding the right balance between getting answers and by not asking leading questions. But at the end the results are really fascinating and it was an awesome experience. Interesting results were found and were implemented in the new iteration, which you’ll find below.

Integration with Tikkie

I needed to make sure the kid understands the message and is familiar with Tikkie. The button opens Tikkie via deeplink. I had to change parts of the copy as well to improve the tone of voice.

Better safe then sorry

There is an extra step-by-step guide, I added this because it wasn’t always clear what the upcoming steps were. To minimise the error’s, while going back to our app it will automatically show the paste button.

Learn, adapt, evolve

It seemed that setting up their blanket via Tikkie was too hard. Because it was experienced as difficult and time wise, we decided it was best to advice the help of a parent. As an extra I’ve made little printed manuals to help out the kid & the parent in setting up their blanket. I’ve designed the blanket in Illustrator and sent the files to a printing house to print these awesome blankets.

Even on a rainy Kingsday and with only eight blankets, we achieved fourteen individual transactions! Which I find a huge accomplishment for a three month project.

My biggest learnings

Kids don't read

I had to make small adjustments to the copy and UI to get them to understand the process. But even so it was hard to let them complete the process. Testing this is complete different story…

Don't give up on implementation

The orange wave background animation was also a struggle to implement. I just started working with Lottie and apparently it doesn’t support effects or expressions (which was the only thing I used). Through trying and failure, together with the developers we made it work. The animation was important, since it made the app visually attractive for kids.
